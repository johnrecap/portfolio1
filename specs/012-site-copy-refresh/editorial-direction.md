# Site Copy Editorial Direction

## Purpose

This guide defines how the public site should sound during the copy refresh so Arabic and English stay aligned in audience, tone, and restraint.

## Audience Priority

### Primary Audience

Clients who may hire Mohamed for product, website, dashboard, or implementation work.

They need to understand:

- what kind of work he does
- what kinds of problems he helps solve
- whether he sounds credible, clear, and easy to work with
- whether contacting him feels worth the time

### Secondary Audience

Hiring teams, collaborators, or peers.

Their needs matter, but they should not shape the main tone of the public site.

## Core Tone

The site should sound:

- personal
- simple
- direct
- professional
- calm
- specific

The site should not sound:

- flashy
- slogan-heavy
- overly polished
- startup-pitchy
- over-friendly
- slangy
- written to impress other developers

## Voice Rules

### 1. Speak like one person, not a brand deck

The writing should feel like Mohamed speaking clearly about his work, not like a generic agency page or AI-generated landing page.

Preferred:

- clear first-person or direct phrasing
- plain statements about what he builds and how he works
- grounded claims tied to real outcomes

Avoid:

- abstract positioning language
- inflated branding language
- empty "vision" phrasing

### 2. Put client understanding before style

Every important section should help a client answer one of these questions:

- What do you build?
- What kind of projects do you take on?
- What is it like to work with you?
- Why should I trust your approach?
- What should I do next?

If a sentence sounds elegant but does not answer a useful question, it should be rewritten or removed.

### 3. Keep confidence without exaggeration

The copy should sound assured, but it should not overclaim.

Preferred:

- "I build..."
- "I help teams..."
- "I focus on..."
- "This site shows..."

Avoid:

- "world-class"
- "cutting-edge"
- "revolutionary"
- "end-to-end excellence"
- "best-in-class"

### 4. Use specific language over broad marketing language

Prefer concrete terms like:

- public site
- dashboard
- internal tool
- product workflow
- frontend
- backend
- content management
- launch
- maintenance

Avoid broad, inflated phrases like:

- digital transformation
- innovative solutions
- seamless experiences
- powerful ecosystem
- comprehensive range of services

### 5. Stay warm, but do not sound casual

The site should feel human and approachable without sounding chatty or colloquial.

Preferred:

- polite
- steady
- easy to read

Avoid:

- jokes
- hype
- filler warmth
- slang
- exaggerated friendliness

## Section-Level Writing Lens

### Hero

Goal:

- explain what Mohamed builds
- show who the work is for
- make the offer legible quickly

Should feel:

- clear
- grounded
- useful

Should avoid:

- cinematic claims
- tech-showoff phrasing
- generic "from idea to launch" wording unless made specific

### About

Goal:

- show how Mohamed thinks about the work
- make his working style feel dependable

Should feel:

- reflective
- practical
- credible

Should avoid:

- self-celebration
- over-explaining basic competence

### Projects

Goal:

- help clients understand what kinds of systems and problems Mohamed has worked on

Should feel:

- informative
- organized
- quiet in tone

Should avoid:

- portfolio cliches
- filler around filters, badges, and empty states

### Contact

Goal:

- make reaching out feel simple and worth doing
- reduce friction and ambiguity

Should feel:

- welcoming
- straightforward
- respectful of the client's time

Should avoid:

- needy CTA language
- vague promises
- forced enthusiasm

## Banned Patterns

These patterns should be removed during implementation unless a sentence has been rewritten into something concrete and natural.

### Generic AI-Sounding Patterns

- "production-ready from day one"
- "battle-tested"
- "comprehensive range"
- "great products"
- "bring your ideas to life"
- "strong engineering partner from concept to launch"
- "seamless"
- "robust and scalable" when unsupported by context

### Empty Portfolio Phrases

- "I turn ideas into useful digital products" when not followed by specific meaning
- "building modern experiences" without saying what kind
- "clean architecture" without client relevance
- "product thinking" when used as a vague badge rather than a practical way of working

### Overwritten Arabic Patterns

- ornate phrasing that sounds translated from English
- phrases that over-explain competence in formal language
- emotionally inflated wording around ordinary actions like replying, shipping, or building
- stacked adjectives where one clear phrase would do

### Over-Friendly or Slangy Patterns

- playful slang
- overly intimate phrasing
- exaggerated excitement
- copy that sounds like social media rather than a professional site

## Arabic and English Parity Rules

### 1. Match intent, not wording

Arabic and English should say the same thing in spirit and in value, even if sentence structure changes.

Literal translation is not required.

### 2. Keep the same level of formality

If English is calm and direct, Arabic should also be calm and direct.

Neither version should feel noticeably more promotional, more stiff, or more casual than the other.

### 3. Let Arabic be naturally Arabic

Arabic can reorder ideas, shorten phrasing, or swap sentence structure if that makes the writing sound more native and less translated.

### 4. Do not add claims in one language only

If a claim, promise, or positioning point appears in one language, it should also exist in the other unless there is a deliberate product reason not to.

### 5. Keep UI strings compact

Buttons, labels, tabs, chips, empty states, and headings should stay short enough to fit the existing layout in both languages.

### 6. Prefer clarity over symmetry

The two versions do not need identical rhythm or line length.

They do need:

- equivalent meaning
- equivalent warmth
- equivalent confidence

## Writing Checklist

Before accepting any rewritten line, check:

1. Is this clear on first read?
2. Does this help a client understand something useful?
3. Does this sound like a real person rather than a generic marketing template?
4. Is the claim specific enough to be believable?
5. Would the same line still sound strong if stripped of buzzwords?
6. Does the Arabic version feel written in Arabic, not translated into it?
7. Does the English version avoid startup cliche and portfolio filler?

If the answer to any of these is no, rewrite it.
